# Executive Proof Boundary Checklist

## Purpose

Prevent executive-value material from drifting into unsupported, inflated, or context-stripped claims.

This checklist governs resume evidence, executive briefings, interview stories, publications, opportunity packets, and consulting frameworks derived from the executive-value cluster.

## Claim Types

| Claim Type | Example | Required Boundary |
|---|---|---|
| Quantified financial claim | Revenue, EBITDA, cost reduction | Source document, prior record, or clearly labeled personal claim. |
| Time compression claim | Three years in eighteen months; seven days to seventy-two hours | Source record and context of comparison. |
| Operational stability claim | Zero downtime, transformation continuity | Scope, environment, and evidence boundary. |
| Leadership capability claim | Converts chaos into momentum | Can be framed as positioning if not presented as independently measured fact. |
| Comparative claim | Faster than peers, better than standard approach | Requires benchmark, context, or softened framing. |
| Causal claim | X caused Y business outcome | Requires evidence chain or must be reframed as contribution. |

## Claim Disposition

Every claim receives one disposition:

1. **Citable** — source-backed and safe for external use.
2. **Internal Evidence** — usable for private positioning, not public without support.
3. **Personal Claim** — usable if clearly attributed as personal experience.
4. **Illustrative Framing** — useful as metaphor or model, not proof.
5. **Hold** — insufficient support.
6. **Remove** — likely misleading or too risky.

## Review Questions

For each executive claim, answer:

1. What exactly is being claimed?
2. Who or what is the source?
3. Is the claim quantitative, qualitative, comparative, or causal?
4. What is the scope?
5. What is the timeframe?
6. What evidence supports it?
7. What evidence is missing?
8. Can it be used publicly?
9. Does it need softer wording?
10. Does it strengthen capability or merely decorate positioning?

## Public Wording Rules

Use strong wording when proof is strong.

Use bounded wording when proof is partial.

Avoid:

- unverifiable superlatives,
- vague superiority claims,
- unsupported causality,
- decontextualized numbers,
- implied guarantees.

Prefer:

- proof-bounded outcomes,
- context-rich claims,
- evidence-linked narratives,
- capability framing,
- contribution language where causality is not fully provable.

## Output Surfaces Governed

- Executive briefings
- Resume evidence banks
- Interview stories
- LinkedIn/profile copy
- Consulting diagnostics
- Publications
- Book/manuscript material
- AI context packs
